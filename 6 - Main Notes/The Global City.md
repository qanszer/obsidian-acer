## The Concept of the Global City

The concept of the **"global city"** emerged in the 1980s, building upon the earlier idea of the "world city." These cities serve as crucial nodes in the global economic system, producing specialized financial and producer services essential for the functioning of the globalized economy.

Global cities wield significant influence across economic, cultural, and political spheres, serving as vital hubs for international finance, trade, transportation, and communication networks.

### By Saskia Sassen

- Economic activities across different areas serve as a key factor for growth and importance of central corporate functions.
- Central functions are bought from highly specialized service firms.
- Specialized service firms engaged in the most complex and globalized markets are subject to agglomeration economies.
- Outsourcing enables firms to grow and move to different locations.
- Specialized service firms need to provide global service through global networks.
- There is a disconnection between cities and their national economies.
- Cities find their effective demand through the growing informalization of a range of economic activities.

> Global cities are important locations that oversee and contribute to the global economy.

## Major Global Cities

### New York
Home to the **New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)** — the largest stock exchange in the world by market capitalization, located in the Financial District of Lower Manhattan.

### London
Home to the **Financial Times Stock Exchange (FTSE)** — a stock market index of 100 of the most highly capitalised blue chip companies listed on the London Stock Exchange.

### Tokyo
Home to the **Nikkei 225** — a stock market index for the Tokyo Stock Exchange measuring the performance of 225 large, publicly owned companies across a wide array of industry sectors.

## What Makes Cities Global?

1. **Trade and Connectivity** — Foster city development and draw people in.
2. **Open, Diverse, and Entrepreneurial Populations** — Spawn sharing of knowledge and ideas.
3. **Innovation and Influence** — From invention and advance.
4. **Discovery of New Markets** — Propelled by inventions.
5. **Geopolitical Change** — Ability to take advantage of geopolitical change and be ready for the next cycle.

## Indicators of a Global City

### Economic Power
The foremost characteristic of a global city — determines which city is global.
- New York has the largest stock market in the world.
- Tokyo houses the greatest number of corporate headquarters.

### Economic Opportunities
Economic opportunities in a global city make it attractive to talents from across the world — the reason why excellent and competent workers/professionals move to global cities.

### Economic Competitiveness
The Economist Intelligence Unit uses criteria like market size, purchasing power of citizens, size of the middle class, and potential growth to determine economic competitiveness.

### Center of Authority
A global city does not solely rely on economic power — it is also reliant on state power.

### Political Influence
Countries that house major international organizations are centers of political influence.
- United Nations headquarters — New York
- European Union headquarters — Brussels
- ASEAN headquarters — Jakarta
- European Central Bank — Frankfurt

### Center of Higher Learning and Culture
A city's intellectual influence is seen through its publishing industry. Many books are published in New York, London, and Paris. Many students move to countries with leading universities.
