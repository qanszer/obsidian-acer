# The Scarlet Worm's Relation to The Gospel

In Psalm 22, a messianic prophecy Jesus quoted from the cross, we read these haunting words:

“I am a worm and not a man, scorned by mankind and despised by the people.”
Psalm 22:6

The Hebrew word for worm is tola'ath—a term associated with the scarlet worm in ancient imagery.

The picture is powerful.

The female attaches herself to wood as she brings forth life. In the process, her body is crushed, and the wood is stained crimson. Her offspring are covered by what comes from her, marked by it, protected by it, sustained because of it. Then she dies. And in time, what remains turns white.

Then Isaiah gives us words that seem to echo that very mystery:

“Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow.”
Isaiah 1:18

This is the Gospel in shadow form.
Jesus was lifted up on wood.

His body was broken.
His blood was poured out.

And by His death, life was given to those covered by His sacrifice.
What was scarlet with sin is made white through Him.

This is why the cross was never an accident.
And this is why Scripture is unlike any other book.

Long before Calvary, God was already preaching Christ.
The worm was not random.
The image was not accidental.
Creation itself was whispering the Gospel before the nails were ever driven.

To those concerned that the 'crimson worm' imagery is a stretch of the truth: the Hebrew text and archaeological record actually confirm it.

The Hebrew word tola’ath is not a generic term for a garden worm. It refers specifically to the Crimson Scale Insect (Kermes vermilio).

Archaeologists have found pieces of fabric in the very lands where the Bible was written - dating back nearly 4,000 years - that were dyed with this exact crimson worm. This isn't just a nice story; it is a historical fact that the people of Israel used this specific 'worm' to create the scarlet colour for the Tabernacle.

Further, the depth of this "crimson worm" imagery is revealed in the poetic contrast of Job 25:6:

“How much less man, who is a maggot (rimmah), and the son of man, who is a worm (tola’ath)?”

Here, Scripture uses two distinct words to define our condition and His sacrifice.
- We are the rimmah: the maggot of decay and spiritual corruption.
- But the "Son of Man" became the tola’ath.
While we were worthless in our sin, He became the lowly "crimson grub" that must be crushed to produce the dye. He was despised as a worm so that His blood could cover the maggot.

Interestingly enough, it turns white 3 days after, like how Jesus rose from the dead after 3 days
